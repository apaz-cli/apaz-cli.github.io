Introduction¶

What is Economic Growth?¶

Economic growth is used by Economists in many ways. They use it to adjust future expectations and measure the efficiency of the country's governance and market total output. Economic growth can be measured by the potential and actual output, and output can be measured by GDP, or Gross Domestic Product. GDP is comprised of consumption spending, investment, government expenditures, and net exports. There are many things that can hinder or help the development of economic growth, and therefore the country's growth. These are things such as:

  • Openness of the market
  • Labor force and labor force participation rate
  • Capital
    • human
    • physical
  • Relative income
  • Depreciation
  • Investment
  • Technology
  • Population and population growth
  • Efficiency
  • Productivity
  • Governance
    • rule of law
    • ease of business
    • patent creation

What is the Ramsey Model?¶

The Ramsey–Cass–Koopmans model, or Ramsey growth model, is a neoclassical model of economic growth based primarily on the work of Frank P. Ramsey. The Ramsey Model includes a system of equations for Capital (represented by K) and Consumption (represented by C). Together, we try to model the optimal growth of the economy so that we can optimize consumption and capital in order to reach the steady state solutions. Within these models, we can add functions or variables that acount for behavioral economics such as commitment technologies, present bias, and/or discounting. By the end of this study, we added these things to the Ramsey Model:

  • population growth, $n$
    • This effects economic growth because it effects output per capita.
  • the depreciation of capital, $\delta$
    • where "depreciation" means diminishing value over time
    • For physical capital, this is like buildings or roads needing repairs and their value diminishing over time.
    • For human capital, it can be things like aging and then going into retirement or certain skills getting outmoded with time.
  • the cost of technological upkeep, $x$
    • this cost can vary depending whether you are a developing country or a developed country. To be specific, if you are a developed country, you are paving the road for more development which takes a lot of resources like time, effort and money. This is as opposed to trying to catch up to technological advancements that already exist, which is "catching up growth"; this occurs more quickly and is cheaper.
    • To be specific, the so-called cost of technological upkeep is actually the cost of investments into R and D creation, and is actually risky; there is a tradeoff between riskiness and reliability when it comes to choosing to invest in a developed country or a developing country. Unfortunately, it is a lot more reliable to invest in a developed country, despite the growth of developing countries being dependent on the amount of investment put into these countries.
  • Behavioral economic factors
    • present bias, $\phi(t)$, which is valuing our present utility over future utility. It can also be called time preference.
    • discounting, $\frac{\rho}{\beta}$, which means discounting the future cost of actions, such as being more willing to go to the gym tomorrow versus today.
    • how effective present bias is, $\gamma$, as in how much more do we value our present utility</font>
    • time, $t$, because present bias is time-dependent

Modifications of the Ramsey model: a History¶

The Ramsey Model:¶

The Ramsey Model has been created and modified by many people over time. For instance, it is actually called the Ramsey-Cass-Koopmans model because it was originally developed by these three people. From there, it has been edited by both Laibson and Barro.

What the Ramsey Model was initially:¶

$\begin{array}{l} \dot{k}=f(k)-c \\ \dot{c}=c\left(f^{\prime}(k)-\rho\right) \end{array}$

for a given output function, $f(k)$ and long-run rate of time preference, $\rho$. To be clear, the output function is how much output we get given our capital.

using the Cobb-Douglas output function, $f(k) = k^\alpha$, we get:

$\begin{array}{l} \dot{c}=c\left(\alpha k^{\alpha-1}-\rho\right) \\ \dot{k}=k^{\alpha}-c \end{array}$

How do we go about solving this?¶

This is an Initial Value Problem, so we solve it "forwards" or "backwards" given some initial conditions.

For what we call "forwards" solving, these initial conditions are literally the initial values, and specify where we should begin modeling from. In our case, Economists want to find the Policy Function which is the equation that will take us to the steady state solution. This means we do not necessarily know what our initial values are. Therefore, we instead try to work "backwards" from the steady state solution.

This means, for what we call "backwards solving", we perturb, or move, slightly off of the steady state solution by some infinitesimal value and try to solve the equation backwards to find the initial values.

For our equations, we are given the initial k-value, so when solving forwards, we use it and some found c-value, otherwise, when bacwards solving, we try to work backwards from the steady state so that we arrive perfectly at the given k-value, which proves difficult. We will outline throughout this notebook how we went about doing this.

Definining some terms:¶

For clarification, the steady state solution is where the "isoclines", a.k.a. "nullclines", of our system meet. In other words, setting the equations from our system to zero, we solve for k and c, and these resulting k and c equations are our isoclines. We then find where these two lines meet. That point is our steady state solution. Theoretically, it is where k and c are stable and will NOT shoot off towards infinity or crash into zero. To be more clear, we are dealing with an unstable system, so if we are even slightly off of the steady state solution, our so-called solution will shoot off towards infinity or drop to zero, which is not ideal when we are trying to model our economy (for instance, we cannot maintain infinite consumption). Laibson and Baro's Modifications to the Ramsey Model¶

What Laibson had done already:¶

First, Laibson added depreciation of capital, $\delta$, and discounting in the form of $\frac{\rho}{\beta}$, $\rho$ being the long-term rate of time preference, i.e. how does a rational person that is not affected by present bias view this capital, and $\beta$ being the short-term rate of time preference, or how does the person being affected by present bias view it.

$\begin{array}{l} \dot{k}=k^{\alpha}-c-\delta k \\ \dot{c}=c\left(\alpha k^{\alpha-1}-\delta-\frac{\rho}{\beta}\right) \end{array}$

He then continued to add population growth, $n$, technological upkeep, $x$, and the function for present bias, $\phi(t)=be^{-\gamma t}$. He also adds $\lambda$ which represents the "effective" rate of time preference, effective implying we are linearlizing the time preference to find its approximation i.e. how much present bias is effecting our decision. It will be a lot in the short term and less in the long term. $\lambda$ is bounded by ${\rho ≤ \lambda ≤ \rho+𝜙'(0)}$.

Laibson ends up with these systems of equations:¶

Full Commitment: \begin{align} \dot{k} &= k^\alpha - \left(\delta + n + x\right)k - c\\ \dot{c} &= c\left(\alpha k^{\alpha - 1} - \left(\delta + x\right) - \left(\rho + be^{-\gamma t}\right) \right) \end{align}

No Commitment: \begin{align} \dot{k} &= k^\alpha - \left(\delta + n + x\right)k - c\\ \dot{c} &= c\left(\alpha k^{\alpha - 1} - \left(\delta + x + \lambda\right) \right) \end{align}

What Baro added to Laibson's work:¶

Laibson believed it was a discontinuous jump from present bias to no present bias, but Baro made it a continuous line between the two. This makes the model easier to work with.

Solving their Systems of Equations:¶

To solve our systems of equations, we ran into problems with this being an unstable problem as well as the variable of time being within the full commitment case. For the no commitment case, we are able to use something called the Time Elimination method, like what we used above to solve the old Ramsey Model. Briefly described, the time elimination method is the method by which the time variable is effectively removed from the equation as it was implicit in nature and can simply be removed by finding dividing $\dot{k}$ from $\dot{c}$. This is not possible for the full commitment case.

Due to the nature of full commitment not being solvable by the same means no commitment was, the full commitment case must be solved backwards from the equilibrium values found previously through solving the system of equations, the “solution” that will be found by this solver will come at roughly k(0) which we got from the Baro paper to be $.25k^{*}_0$ (this is the equilibrium solution for the non-commitment case of a given system, ex: $k_0^*=2459.49884228438$ by which k(0) is roughly 614.22 for column 1)

By using a “shooting” method alongside a numerical value estimation technique such as the bisection or secant method (by which were chosen due to their non-reliance on taking a derivative as the given equations would not be easy to work with in terms of their derivatives). This means we can therefore “get off” or perturb on both sides of the equilibrium solution (above and below) for c*(inf) (ex: 192.90 for column 1), we can safely narrow down where exactly the initial point must be in order for the given equations to result in that equilibrium solution that was the starting point of the solver. Thus as an example (which may or may not be shown below) for the first column this resulting value is roughly 77.2.

The benefit of this implementation in particular is the only variables that are needed to be given to solve for this value are a final time value (ex: 120 for columns 1-3 seemed to be a good fit) and a maximum iteration count by which is essentially just a limit for how many iterations the solver is required/allowed to do before giving up (by which does not seem to be reached unless set fairly/very low. (Additionally it has been discovered that as the overall range of vallues occuring through the system of equations lowers the boundary by which restricts the intepolation/shooting method needs to be lowered in an equivalent fashion, see next paragraph)

Using the Jacobian for further analysis of the system¶

The Jacobian of the system:¶
$$J(k,c)= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha k^{\alpha-1}-\left(\delta+n+x\right) & -1\\ \alpha(\alpha-1)ck^{\alpha-2} & \alpha k^{\alpha - 1} - \left(\delta + x\right) - \left(\rho + be^{-\gamma t}\right)\\ \end{bmatrix}$$

When the Jacobian is at a steady state the $$\rho + be^{-\gamma t}$$ will be 0 as t approaches infinity.

$$J(k,c)= \begin{bmatrix} \alpha k^{\alpha-1}-\left(\delta+n+x\right) & -1\\ \alpha(\alpha-1)ck^{\alpha-2} & 0\\ \end{bmatrix}$$

Through this we can find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the Jacobian matrix to check the stability of the system as it is centered at the steady state.

This can be analyzed by the converging or diverging of the values to and away from the the centeralized steady point.
